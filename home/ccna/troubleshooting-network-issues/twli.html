Troubleshoot WLAN Issues

Ensuring continuous wireless networking capabilities is another important facet of a CCNA's duties.

Common WLAN Issues

Common WLAN issues include:

  • Weak signal. The client is too far from the WAP.
  • Wrong user authentication. The user is trying to enter the wrong password when trying to connect to the WAP.
  • Standalone AP is too congested or has reached the maximum number of clients it can handle. Most standalone APs and SOHO wireless routers can only handle 10 concurrent client connections at a time.
  • RADIUS server is not authorizing the user or device. A customer might not have paid their subscription, or a device might be violating policy (such as time of day, type of device, user group affiliation, etc.) configured on the RADIUS server.
  • Wrong RADIUS password between WAP and RADIUS server. WLANs that use RADIUS to authenticate users typically also require the WAP to authenticate itself to the RADIUS server. It's possible that the password (secret key) was entered incorrectly when first setting up RADIUS.
  • Frequency mismatch on SOHO networks. The 802.11 standards provide for five distinct frequency ranges which, in turn, are divided into channels. Two of these ranges (or bands), 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz, are the ones most commonly used for Wi-Fi signals. Many newer devices support both bands, but on an established WLAN, 5 GHz devices might not be able to communicate with older 2.4 GHz equipment.
  • Connecting to a WAP when its SSID is hidden. If the SSID is not broadcast, all wireless clients must be configured manually.
  • Security protocol mismatch. The WAP will likely use WPA2 or WPA2 enterprise, so if the OS or drivers on the clients are not compatible with the WAP's security protocol, clients will have problems connecting to and using the WLAN.
  • WAP misconfiguration. In environments with multiple VLANs and SSIDs, it is possible that the AP could be configured with an erroneous setting. This can result in clients not being able to connect to the AP.
  • General network issues at both ends of the connection, including IP address and subnet configuration, default gateway address, and DHCP server address, among others.
  • Wi-Fi disconnect. Users might find that their Wi-Fi connections keep dropping. This could stem from outdated drivers on the client devices, too many clients attempting to connect to the WLAN at the same time, or a device moving in and out of range of WAP radios.
  • LAP too far from controller. Lightweight access points are usually PoE devices. They create a tunnel to their controller to receive commands and pass user traffic. LAPs that are more than 50 meters away from their controller should be connected using CAT6 or better cable, otherwise the voltage drop on the line will be too great and they will have trouble establishing a connection with their WLC.

Guidelines for Troubleshooting WLAN Issues

Here are some guidelines for troubleshooting WLAN issues.

Troubleshooting WLAN Issues

Consider the following when your users experience WLAN issues:

  • Try connecting to a suspect AP with a wireless device that is known to be configured properly for the WLAN.
  • Make sure that enough APs are available for the anticipated number of wireless clients, and add more APs on occasions when there will be a significant increase in the number of wireless users.
  • Verify that the configurations of the APs and controllers do not contain errors.
  • Verify that the client devices are properly configured for the WLAN.
  • Test wireless signal strength by using a tool such as an RF signal meter or an application such as WiFi Analyzer or Wireshark.
  • Locate and correct general network issues.
