Explain The Similarities And Differences Between Network Types

Topic A

Network Types

This lesson introduces basic network concepts that can help you with implementing and administering Cisco networks. Often, the level of complexity of administrative tasks depends on the type of network you are managing. To begin, you will identify network types. In this topic, you will explain the similarities and differences between network types.

The Networking Process

Before you dig into the intricacies of managing networks, take a look at the networking process in general.

Most networks are composed of computers, servers, printers, and connectivity devices, all connected by a shared medium. Regardless of the type of device used, the networking process has four basic components:

  • An application that wants to communicate with another application
  • A common language (protocol) that the two sides use to communicate
  • A network interface that allows the nodes to connect
  • A transmission medium that carries the data from one node to the next

Common Network Types

Networks are often classified into various types based on the area that they cover. The following table describes the most common network types.

Type of Network Description
Local Area Network (LAN) Covers an area that is small enough to fit in a building.
A type of network that covers an area roughly equivalent to a single floor or building.
Metropolitan Area Network (MAN) Covers an area equivalent to a city or a municipality.
Campus Area Network (CAN) Covers an area equivalent to an academic campus or business park and is typically owned or used exclusively by a single entity.
Personal Area Network (PAN) Connects two or three computers with cables and is most often seen in small offices or homes.
Wireless Personal Area Network (WPAN) Is a variation of PAN that connects devices in close proximity through technologies such as Infrared and Bluetooth® but not through a Wireless Access Point (WAP).
Wide Area Network (WAN) Covers a large area and is usually spread across geographical locations.

Local Area Networks (LANs)

A LAN is a self-contained network that:

  • Spans a small area such as a single building, floor, or room.
  • Provides fast data transfer.
  • Does not require a leased telecommunication system for its functioning.
  • Includes multiple nodes and segments, which are directly connected with cables or short-range wireless technologies.
  • Can be used to implement different technologies depending on the configuration and operational requirements.

Wide Area Networks (WANs)

A WAN:

  • Provides access outside of the LAN.
  • Provides connectivity between two or more locations over long distances.
  • Connects disparate LANs to make one large intranet to facilitate corporate communications between branch locations.
  • Can be private, such as those built and maintained by large, multinational corporations, or can be public, such as the Internet.

Metropolitan Area Networks (MANs) are specialized types of WANs.

Connectivity Options

You can also classify network types based on the sort of connectivity that is present. The primary connectivity option are:

  • Wired network: A type of network that uses different types of cabling to establish connectivity among nodes and enable data transfer and communication among the nodes.
  • Wireless network: A type of network that allows nodes to connect to each other, communicate, and transfer data within the network without using any physical media.

Wired Networks

Wired networks typically use either copper or fiber optic cabling (or a combination of both) to connect network devices and nodes.

  • The most widely used copper cabling today is designed in accordance with the IEEE 802.3 standard, which is commonly known as Ethernet. Ethernet is a set of networking technologies and media access methods specified for LANs. The original Ethernet standard described specifications for copper cabling, but ammendments to the standard also define specifications for other transmission media, including optical fiber and wireless communications.
  • Fiber optic cables were traditionally used more for long-distance connections and network backbones, but as the cost of implementing and maintaining fiber optic cables decrease, their use in short-distance communications increases.

There are several types of Ethernet that use copper and fiber optic media, as described in the following table.

Ethernet Type Description
Ethernet A LAN technology that connects computers by using switches.
Fast Ethernet An Ethernet technology that can transmit data along either copper or fiber optic cable at speeds up to 100 Mbps. The maximum cable length for copper is 100 m (328 feet) and ranges from 400 m (1312 feet) to 2 km (1.24 miles) for fiber optic cables.
Gigabit Ethernet (GbE) An Ethernet technology that can transmit data at speeds up to 1000 Mbps. Copper cable can run up to 100 m (328 feet), while fiber optic cables are used for distances ranging from 500 m (1640 feet) to 100 km (62 miles).
10 Gigabit Ethernet (10GbE) An Ethernet technology that can transmit data at speeds up to 10 Gbps. Depending on the grade of copper, maximum cable length is either 55 m (180 feet) or 100 m (328 feet). Depending on the type of fiber optic cable, maximum distances range from 300 m (984 feet) to 40 km (25 miles)
40 Gigabit Ethernet (40GbE) An Ethernet technology that can transmit data at speeds up to 40 Gbps. The defining characteristic is the use of two transceivers connected by a OM3 or OM4 fiber optic cable, with each transceiver being connected to a network server or other device. Maximum cable lengths depend on the type of cable and transceiver used.
100 Gigabit Ethernet (100GbE) An Ethernet technology that can transmit data at speeds up to 100 Gbps. This technology also uses two transceivers connected by OM3 or OM4 fiber. Maximum cable lengths depend on the type of cable and transceiver used, ranging from 5 m (16 feet) to over 80 km (50 miles).

Wireless Networks

A wireless network:

  • Typically consists of client systems such as desktop PCs, laptops, or tablets and wireless connectivity devices such as access points (APs) or Internet of Things (IoT) devices.
  • Enables users to connect to a local network or the Internet, even on the move.
  • Can be easier to install and configure than a wired network and affords clients a lot of mobility.
  • Is useful in areas where it would be difficult or impossible to connect using wires, such as in hazardous areas, across long distances, or inside historic buildings.
  • Can introduce security concerns if APs are not configured to limit traffic to authorized users.
