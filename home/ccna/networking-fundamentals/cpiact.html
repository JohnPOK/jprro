Compare Physical Interface and Cabling Types

Topic F

Tranmission Media and Connectors

Due to the open nature of the OSI model and the TCP/IP model, there are many options for carrying networking signals. As a CCNA, you are expected to distinguish between different transmission media and physical interfaces.

Tranmission Media

A transmission medium is a physical path for electrical, optical, or electromagnetic signals to travel for data transfer from a sender to a receiver. Two main types of tranmission media are commonly used: bounded (wired) and unbounded (wireless).

Physical Media

Physical media are bounded transmission media that are normally composed of copper or glass cables and their connectors.

Copper Cabling

Copper cable is a type of physical media that is composed of strands of copper wire that are either twisted around each other (twisted pair) or enclosed in layers of insulation and copper mesh (coaxial).

Twisted pair cables can be further categorized as unshielded or shielded.

  • Unshielded twisted pair (UTP) cable is the most common type of cable used for Ethernet networks with speeds up to 10 Gbps. Four wire pairs are twisted around each other to minimize electromagnetic interference and enclosed in a plastic sheath.
  • Shielded twisted pair (STP) cable are found in high-speed networks. For 10 Gbps Ethernet distances greater than 55 meters, STP must be used. Like UTP, four wire pairs are twisted around each other, but a metal shield encloses the wires (for additional protection from interference), and then the entire cable is enclosed in a plastic sheath.

To ensure that the building or campus infrastructure is able to meet the needs of deploying both 100 and 1000 Mbps Ethernet, the cabling you use must adhere to specifications defined by the Electronic Industries Association (EIA) and the Telecommunications Industry Association (TIA). The standards vary for each type of Ethernet and define areas such as connector type, maximum cable length, and the type of physical media used.

Copper Connectors

Copper connectors are placed at the ends of copper cables to provide an interface for connecting to electronic devices such as computers, switches, and broadband routers.

  • Twisted pair connectors are called RJ-45 connectors and resemble a telephone jack.
  • Coaxial connectors used in networking are called BNC connectors, which are round metal connectors that either have a protruding metal pin or a receptacle to receive the pin.

Power over Ethernet

Power over Ethernet (PoE) is the practice of using regular twisted pair cabling to carry both DC electricity and Ethernet data. Typically, PoE is used where it is not practical to install an electrical power supply for devices such as wireless APs, IP cameras, IP phones, and keyless entry pads. PoE can be supplied from a PoE-capable switch, or added to the line by a PoE injector. Most PoE implementations supply 48 volts DC at 15.4 watts per device.

  • Note: Both the device and the switch must support PoE.

Fiber Optic Cabling

Fiber optic cable is a type of physical media that is composed of ultra-thin strands of glass or plastic through which light can pass. It supports much longer distances than the 100 meters supported by Ethernet on UTP cables. It also experiences much less interference from outside sources when compared to copper cables.

The two main types of fiber optic cabling are:

  • Single-mode fiber
  • Multimode fiber

These correspond to the propagation mode, which is a characteristic of light waves that refers to how the light travels through a medium such as optical fiber. Variations in the intensity of the light over the cable's cross section and down its length are caused by reflection and refraction, and the number of modes in a given cable depend on the cable's dimensions and the amount of refraction that occurs in the core and cladding.

Single-Mode Fiber

Single-mode fiber (SMF) is an optical fiber designed to carry light directly down the fiber in only one propagation mode. This enables the signal to travel longer distances, but is more expensive than multimode fiber and is used with laser-based hardware for long-distance high-bandwidth connections such as those implemented by telephone companies, cable television providers, and higher education.

Multimode Fiber

Multimode fiber (MMF) is an optical fiber designed to carry light directly down the fiber in more than one propagation mode. This cable type has a larger core than SMF (enabling multiple signals to be transmitted), but more signals are lost over longer distances. It is generally cheaper than SMF and it works fine with less-expensive LEDs in LANs.

Fiber Optic Connectors

Fiber optic connectors provide the interfaces for fiber optic cables to be connected to network devices or nodes. There are many connectors available, but the most common are ST, SC, FC, MT-RJ, and LC, each of which can be used with single-mode or multimode fiber.

Connector Type Description
ST (Straight Tip)

  • Are similar in appearance to BNC connectors.
  • Are generally used to connect multimode fibers.
  • Have a straight, ceramic center pin and bayonet lug lockdown.
  • Are often used in network patch panels.
  • Are among the most popular types of fiber connectors.
SC (Subscriber Connector)

  • Are box-shaped connectors that snap into a receptacle.
  • Are often used in a duplex configuration where two fibers are terminated into two SC connectors that are molded together.
  • Are generally used with single-mode fiber.
FC (Ferrule Core)

  • Use a heavy duty ferrule in the center for better mechanical stability than ST connectors. A ferrule is a tubular structure made of ceramic or metal that aligns and protects the stripped end of an optical fiber.
  • Are popular in industrial settings where greater strength and durability is required.
MT-RJ (Mechanical Transfer-Registered Jack)

  • Are sometimes referred to as Fiber Jack connectors.
  • Are compact snap-to-lock connectors, making them easy to use.
  • Are normally used with multimode fiber.
  • Are similar in size to the RJ-45 connector.
  • Connect two strands of fiber.
LC (Lucent Connector)

  • Are sometimes referred to as "Little Connectors".
  • Are used for both single-mode and multimode fiber.
  • Has a small form factor ceramic ferrule. (It is about half the size of an SC or ST connector.)
  • Use an RJ-45-type latching.
  • Can be used to transition installations from twisted pair (copper) cabling to fiber.

Wireless Communication Methods

Wireless communication is another term used for unguided transmission, and common methods include the use of:

  • Radio waves
  • Microwaves
  • Infrared light

Radio Frequency (RF) Communications

RF (radio frequency) communication uses unguided media in the form of radio waves to transmit data from sender to receiver. The term RF refers to the rate of oscillation of electrical current in the range of 20 kiloHertz (kHz) to 300 gigaHertz (gHz).

Wi-Fi

Wi-Fi (wireless fidelity) is a subset of RF communications that is based on IEEE 802.11 standards and enables the creation and use of wireless LANs (WLANs).
